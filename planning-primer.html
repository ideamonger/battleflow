Battleflow Operational Planning Primer

How simulation logic and COA analysis support planning and execution.

Battleflow helps planners reason through conflict as an interaction between two adaptive forces in terrain, time, and uncertainty. It does not claim to predict combat with perfect fidelity. Its purpose is more practical: give staff and decision-makers a transparent way to convert an order of battle, a terrain model, and competing courses of action into a wargame that exposes timing, risk, decision points, and branch requirements before execution.

From Laydown to Executable State

The simulation starts from a human-readable ontology. Blue and Red units are organized by hierarchy, location, frontage, depth, subunit count, weapons, supplies, morale, readiness, and initial orders. Terrain is represented as a grid with land, water, and air conditions: altitude, accessibility, soil, use, sea state, currents, wind, visibility, and weather. This turns a planning packet into a shared operational state that can be inspected, edited, and challenged by both players.

Once the initial laydown is set, Battleflow enforces feasible starting areas, then lets players issue orders at the chosen command echelon or above. Each order specifies where to go, when to start, when to arrive, what to do, and what fallback action to take if timing fails. This keeps the simulation tied to executable command logic rather than abstract movement alone.

Battleflow Simulation Logic

At each time step, units move toward assigned destinations, consume supplies, and interact with terrain. Movement speed is shaped by mobility, readiness, morale, fuel, slope-like accessibility effects, sea state, and air/weather conditions. Engagements are resolved with transparent relative scores using weapon range, rate of fire, destructive power, ammunition, attacker readiness, target cover, and terrain modifiers.

The engine updates strength, readiness, morale, ammunition, fuel, sustainment, objective control, and event logs. The result is not a black-box adjudication. Planners can see why a unit slowed, why a beachhead became contested, why a fire unit remained dangerous, or why a missed timing requirement triggered a fallback branch.

Course of Action Analysis

Battleflow combines the running simulation with COA comparison. In the default Operation IRON TIDE scenario, COAs represent three planning postures: a high-tempo airfield thrust, a lodgment-first maximin option, and a hedged two-axis pressure option. Each is evaluated against Red responses: likely, most dangerous, deceptive, and adaptive.

The COA panel calculates expected value, worst-case floor, worst regret, risk, and composite score. This gives planners three complementary views: expected utility for opportunity, maximin logic for survivability, and minimax-regret logic for robustness when Red behavior is uncertain. As the simulation changes the operational state, COA scores change too, tying the analysis to execution conditions rather than a static slide.

Support to Planning and Execution

Planning

Battleflow turns assumptions into testable variables: force placement, logistics, readiness, terrain friction, fires survival, C2 resilience, and objective value. This helps a staff identify fragile assumptions, compare branches, and choose the next best wargame test.

Execution

During execution rehearsal, Battleflow highlights timing failures, contested objectives, degradation of combat power, logistics stress, and changes in Red threat survival. These indicators support decision points such as continue, pause, shift main effort, reinforce, defend, or withdraw.

Bottom Line

Battleflow is useful because it connects three things that are often separated: a concrete order of battle, a terrain-aware simulation, and game-theoretic COA analysis. That connection lets planners see whether a plan remains coherent after Red acts, terrain interferes, logistics degrade, and timing slips. The system supports operational judgment by making assumptions visible, choices comparable, and branches easier to rehearse before the moment of decision.
